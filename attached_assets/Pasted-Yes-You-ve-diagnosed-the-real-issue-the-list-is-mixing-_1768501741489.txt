Yes. You’ve diagnosed the real issue: **the list is mixing two different semantic classes**, and Farm is explicitly ignoring it anyway — so it’s currently useless and confusing.

### The simple, correct fix

Split this into **two explicit policy lists** and make both Farm + AOD follow them.

## 1) `excluded_vendor_roots`

These are “umbrella” roots that are **too broad to be assets** and should be excluded (or treated as infrastructure/noise):

Examples (likely):

* `microsoft.com`
* `google.com`
* `amazon.com`
* `apple.com`

**Behavior:** never catalog as a standalone asset key (unless you *explicitly* want it in a special bucket).

## 2) `valid_saas_root_domains`

These are legitimate product domains that **are** real SaaS assets and should remain admissible:

Examples:

* `slack.com`
* `dropbox.com`
* `zoom.com` / `zoom.us` (depending)
* `atlassian.com` (debatable, but likely yes)
* `hubspot.com` (often yes)
* `servicenow.com` (often yes)
* `salesforce.com` (often yes)

**Behavior:** treat like normal domains (subject to discovery/governance rules).

---

### Why this matters for your mismatches

* AOD excluding `microsoft.com` / `google.com` is reasonable: they’re not “apps,” they’re corporate umbrellas.
* AOD cataloging `slack.com` / `dropbox.com` is also reasonable: those are direct SaaS products.

So the inconsistency isn’t a bug in AOD — it’s a **bad policy taxonomy**.

---

### One more necessary step

Farm currently says:

> corporate_root_domains intentionally NOT checked

That has to change. Otherwise policy is theater.

So:

* remove/replace `corporate_root_domains`
* implement `excluded_vendor_roots` and actually enforce it in Farm expected-block generation

---

### Bottom line

**Yes**, create `excluded_vendor_roots` (or similar) and keep product domains separate.
Then make both systems obey it via switchboard so you stop getting “key normalization bugs” that are really “intentional exclusions.”
